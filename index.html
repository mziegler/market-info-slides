Shortcomings

  • Usability problems
  • Lack of alternative markets
  • Farmers already know prices
  • Unfamiliarity with other markets
  • Relationships with market agents
  • Attitudes towards risk
"...our informants consistently disclaimed any practice of acquiring market price information for the purpose of comparison between markets (by phone or other means)..."
— Burrell and Oreglia 2015

Interviews

  • 11 farmers in 6 villages
  • 6 Loop aggregators (also farmers)
  • 3 Commission agents
  •  
  • All men, ages 20 to 61
  • 18 of 20 had mobile phones
  • All farmers grew vegetables
  • Majority owned farmland
  •  
  • Interviews in Hindi
  • 2-person interview team
  • Tried for private interview locations
  • All recorded except commission agents
  • Questions informed by prior fieldwork

Most farmers sought and used price information

"I call and keep a check on prices on frequently. Different times during the day, I call different markets... Every day, different markets, different times."

(+ many exceptions)

Many exceptions: trusting local traders, choosing markets based on volume but not price, my local market has good rates.

Information sources

  • Calling commission agents
    "Twenty to twenty five farmers call me every day... we never discuss quantities, only prices."
  • Making deals with traders over the phone
    • Market traders + farm-gate traders
  • Other farmers

Market Choice

"When the mandi starts everyday, [I find] out the rates everyday. Wherever the rate is good, I go there."

Negotiation

"we negotiate with farm-gate traders based on the previous day’s market rates. We offer 2 rupees above yesterday’s rate, and then the traders negotiate it down to 1 rupee above yesterday’s rate"

Arbitrage

Market commission agent:
"Traders call every morning for prices"

Changes since approx. 2015:

  • Mobile phones became widespread
  • Major improvements in roads
  •  
  • Increase in trader numbers, particularly farmgate
  • Changes in farmers' market choices
    ”the traders from Ara mandi are coming here now, so there is no point in going to Ara mandi.”
  • Price stabalization

Consistence with information asymmetry theory:

“Before having mobile phones, traders had better margins because not many people had price information. Now everybody knows the prices.”

Consistence with information scarcity theory:

“Now after mobile phones, prices have become more stable and balanced between markets.”

Localized research is important

Not just prices

Many other factors go into marketing decisions
Market Capacity, Supply Gluts, Price Crashes
"If the local market has more harvest here than we go to distant markets. I have learned a lot selling vegetables over the years. Let’s say I have 2 quintal of tomatoes, and if I get to know someone else has 5 quintal then I know that the rate for tomatoes is going to drop. It’s a competition cycle.""
Production volume and seasonality
"I never take any decision on prices. When the volume is low I go to Dumri market, when it’s high I go to Nayabhojpur market."

Time and Convenience

On the convenience of farm-gate traders:
“Now that these people do it, I just give it to them... I could not manage with everything. I am growing old.”
Selling at a market can take hours

Familiarity

"The farmers need to go to the markets and study. The customers, the prices, the kind and quality of harvest."
...
"If farmers are not well-informed and only make decisions on the basis of prices, it will not be good because every market has different operations."

Relationships with Commission Agents

“All goes wrong if you keep switching commission agents”.

Attitudes towards risk

“Yeah, I have had to return home with the stock... If today I get a bad rate, I might also get a good rate tomorrow.”

Credit

Q: Does the commission agent ever give you money [credit] to buy vegetables or something alke?

A: He does, but we don't take because then we will be tied [to him].

Danger

“There was a problem with the police that resulted in a hassle to go there. On the way, there are two police vans by the road in the night checkpoint and they ask for ₹200–500. There are about 10–15 such checkpoints. You end up giving about ₹3000 for the vehicle and about ₹1500 to the police so any difference in market prices is a loss to us.”

Other strategies

Some market-access barriers are not addressable by a market-information service
  • Time + Distance: Loop aggregation and digital payments
  • Unfamiliarity: farmer networking, market field trips
  • Marketing skills: training videos and courses
